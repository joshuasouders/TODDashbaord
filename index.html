About Transit Oriented Development

MTA’s Station Access and Development Dashboard is an online tool that assesses rail station potential different forms of transit oriented development (TOD). A key driver of increased transit ridership in Maryland, TOD capitalizes on existing rapid transit infrastructure. The online tool focuses on the MTA’s existing MARC Commuter Rail, Metro Subway, and Central Light Rail lines and includes information specific to each station.

The goal of the Station Access and Development Dashboard is to give MTA planning staff, developers, local governments, and transit riders a picture of how each MTA rail station could attract TOD investment. In order to make this assessment, MTA staff gathered data on characteristics that are likely to influence TOD potential. The station-specific data is organized into 6 different categories referring to transit activity; station facilities; parking provision and utilization; bicycle and pedestrian access; and local zoning and land availability around each station.

As a publicly shared resource, the Station Access and Development Dashboard can be used by local communities to identify and prioritize area improvements in coordination with the MTA that can help attract investment around rail stations.

How to Use

The MTA Station Access and Development Dashboard has two components: the interactive map and the station-specific data. The map pane (right) can be navigated in order to select different MTA rail stations. Selecting a station will center the map navigation on that station and activate the information pane (left) of the tool to display the corresponding station information.

The information pane has three tabs and is designed to first display summary information for each data category. The Summary Information tab’s rating of each data category is intended to provide a quick overview understanding of the data. Further explanation of each data category and summary rating calculation is displayed by clicking on a category name. The calculations of the rating system will evolve as the MTA continues to explore the influences and trends of TOD in Maryland.

The detailed data associated with each station can be viewed by clicking on the Data tab within the information pane. This data is updated regularly, and is available for downloaded at the Maryland StateStat Open Data Portal. Included within the station specific data are helpful map layers that can be activated by checking the associated boxes in the information pane.

The Development Resources tab includes helpful links for those who are interested in pursuing transit oriented development projects near MTA stations. These resources include overall MTA, SHA, and MDOT development guidelines as well as station specific area plans when available.

Methodology

  Transit Score

The Transit Score is calculated in 4 steps. First, the stations are ranked by each of the following data points. Second, the station’s ranking for each data point is divided into 5 categories, or scores, ranging from the top 20% of values (5) to the lowest 20% of values (1). Third, the scores for each data point at a station are added up to create the cumulative score at each station. Finally, the stations are ranked by this cumulative score and divided into 5 categories, ranging from the top 20% (5) to the lowest 20% (1). Those values, 1-5, are the Transit Score for each station.

  Transit Data Points*
  • Number of lines and modes served at each station
  • Number of local bus routes served by each station
  • Total average weekday ridership for rail service at each station**
  • Number of trains per weekday serving the station
  • Number of trains per weekend day serving the station
*Each of the data points is equally weighted in calculating the summary score for each station. ** Ridership is calculated the following ways:
  • Metro Rail ridership is based on Metro gate exit counts.
  • Light Rail ridership is estimated using a statistical sampling process in line with FTA established guidelines, and approved by the FTA.
  • MARC ridership is calculated using two (2) independent methods.
    • Monthly Line level ridership is estimated using a statistical sampling process in line with FTA established guidelines, and approved by the FTA. This method of ridership calculation is used by the MTA for official reporting purposes to State level and Federal level reporting.
    • Station level ridership is estimated by using person counts completed by the third party vendor. This method of calculation has not been verified by the FTA for statistical reporting and is used for scheduling purposes only. However, because of the granularity of detail, this information is useful for TOD applications.
    • *Please note that the monthly level ridership and the station level ridership are calculated using two (2) independent methods that are not interchangeable and should not be compared for analysis purposes.


  Station Facility Score

The Station Facility Score is calculated in 3 steps. First, each station is given a 1 or 0 on each of the following criteria. A ‘1’ indicates that a station has a certain feature, and a ‘0’ indicates that it is lacking that feature. Second, the sum of each of the values is added up to create a cumulative score for each station. Third, the stations are ranked by this cumulative score and divided into 5 categories, ranging from the top 20% (5) to the lowest 20% (1). Those values, 1-5, are the Station Facility Score for each station.

  Station Facility Criteria*
  • Does the station have schedule information on display?
  • Does the station have route information on display?
  • Does the station have a ticket booth or ticket machine?
  • Does the station have a shelter for passengers?
  • Does the station have benches for passengers?
  • Are there public restrooms at the station?
  • Are there public phones at the station?
*Each of the data points is equally weighted in calculating the summary score for each station.

  Parking Score

The parking score is calculated in 5 steps. First, the stations are ranked by each of the following data points. Second, the station’s ranking for each data point is divided into 5 categories, or scores, ranging from the top 20% of values (5) to the lowest 20% of values (1). Third, the scores for each data point at a station are added up to create the cumulative score at each station. Fourth, an additional 5 points is added to the cumulative score if there is a fee for parking at the station. Finally, the stations are ranked by this cumulative score and divided into 5 categories, ranging from the top 20% (5) to the lowest 20% (1). Those values, 1-5, are the Parking Score for each station.

  Parking data points
  • Number of parking spots (excluding ADA)
  • Number of ADA parking spots
  • Combined number of total Electric Vehicle Charging (EVC) stations available and occupied EVC spaces (occupied EVC spaces are therefore counted twice)
  • Parking utilization rate (2014)


  Bike Access Score

The Bike Access Score is calculated in 2 steps. First, the sum of each of the data points is added up to create a cumulative score for each station. Second, the stations are ranked by this cumulative score and divided into 5 categories, ranging from the top 20% (5) to the lowest 20% (1). Those values, 1-5, are the Bike Access Score for each station.

  Bike Access data points
  • Number of bicycle racks (all kinds)
  • Number of covered bicycle racks
  • Number of bicycle lockers*
  • Number of occupied bicycle lockers
  • The Short Trip Opportunity Analysis score**
*The number of bicycle lockers is given a weight of .5
**The Short Trip Opportunity Analysis was a zip-code-level GIS analysis completed by Maryland DOT in preparation of their Bike/Ped Master Plan. It is given a weight of 2.

  Pedestrian Access Score

The Pedestrian Access Score is calculated in 4 steps. First, the number of intersections within a half-mile buffer (1 mile diameter) around each station is calculated. Second, the stations are ranked by the number of intersections within their buffer and divided into 5 categories, ranging from the top 20% (5) to the lowest 20% (1). Third, those values, 1-5, are added to the Short Trip Opportunity Analysis (STOA) score for each station’s cumulative score. Finally, the stations are ranked by this cumulative score and divided into 5 categories, ranging from the top 20% (5) to the lowest 20% (1). Those values, 1-5, are the Pedestrian Access Score for each station.

  Pedestrian Access data points
  • Number of intersections within half mile buffer (1 mile diameter) of station
  • The Short Trip Opportunity Analysis score*
*The Short Trip Opportunity Analysis was a zip-code-level GIS analysis completed by Maryland DOT in preparation of their Bike/Ped Master Plan.

  TOD Zoning Score

The TOD Zoning Score is calculated in 3 steps. First, each station is given a score for the number of uses permitted by the zoning code within a half-mile buffer (1 mile diameter) around the station. These uses include Residential, Office, Retail, Manufacturing, and Institutional. A point is earned with each permitted use, with a maximum score of 5. Second, 3 points are given if a station is within a Locally Designated TOD area and 3 additional points are awarded is a station lies in a State Designated TOD area. The resulting score is the cumulative score for each station. Finally, the stations are ranked by this cumulative score and divided into 5 categories, ranging from the top 20% (5) to the lowest 20% (1). Those values, 1-5, are the TOD Zoning Score for each station.

  TOD Zoning data points
  • Number of permitted uses within ½ mile buffer (1 mile diameter) around station
  • Local status as a designated TOD area
  • State status as a designated TOD area


  Development Market Score

The Development Market Score is calculated in two different ways:
1. Within the Baltimore Metropolitan Council (BMC) region (Anne Arundel County, Baltimore City, Baltimore County, Harford County, and Howard County), the score is calculated in 4 steps. First, the stations are ranked by each of the following data points. Second, the station’s ranking for each data point is divided into 5 categories, or scores, ranging from the top 20% of values (5) to the lowest 20% of values (1). Third, the scores for each data point at a station are added up to create the cumulative score at each station. Finally, the stations are ranked by this cumulative score and divided into 5 categories, ranging from the top 20% (5) to the lowest 20% (1). Those values, 1-5, are the Transit Score for each station.

  Building Permit Data Points*:
  • Number of permits issued for residential alterations
  • Number of housing units created by residential alteration permits
  • Number of permits issued for residential new construction
  • Number of housing units created by residential new construction permits
  • Number of permits issued for non-residential alterations
  • Number of permits issued for non-residential new construction
  • Number of permits issued for mixed-use new construction
  • Number of housing units created by mixed-use new construction permits
*Data on permits and housing units comes from the Baltimore Metropolitan Council’s Building Permit Data System. The data on permits and housing units is from 2000 through 2014. Each of the data points is equally weighted in calculating the summary score for each station.

2. Outside of the BMC region, the Development Market score is calculated using data from Maryland Property View. The parcel attributes within Maryland Property View that are utilized are the YEARBLT (year in which the structure was built) and SQFTSTRC (square feet of structure). First, a score is calculated for both the YEARBLT and SQFTSTRC attributes (see below). Second, the YEARBLT score is multiplied by the SQFTSTRC score in order to create a composite score. Third, the sum of the composite scores for all parcels within a ½ mile of the station is calculated in order to create a cumulative composite score for each station. Finally, the range of cumulative composite scores is divided into quintiles. The highest quintile receives a score of “5” and the lowest quintile a score of “1”. Those values, 1-5, are the Market Development Score for each station.
  • YEARBLT Score: Dates more recent than 2011 are given a “5”, dates greater than 2008 are given a “4”, dates greater than 2005 are given a “3”, dates greater than 2002 are given a “2”, dates greater than 1999 are given a “1”, and the remaining parcels are given a score of “0”. Note: Some parcels in Maryland Property View have a value of “0” for YEARBLT. All of these parcels receive a “1” for YEARBLT Score.
  • SQSFTSTRC Score: The values of SQSFTSTRC for each parcel are ranked and divided into 5 categories, ranging from the top 20% (5) to the lowest 20% (1). Those values, 1-5, are the TOD Zoning Score for each station. Note: Some parcels in Maryland Property View have a value of “0” for SQSFTSTRC. All of these parcels receive a “1” for SQSFTSTRC Score.
